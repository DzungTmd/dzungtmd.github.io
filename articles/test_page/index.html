On Finite-Time Control with
Time Transformation Function

The Problem

Given the dynamics \[\begin{align} \dot{x}(t) = \alpha(t) f(x) + d(x) + g(t) , \label{general} \tag{1} \end{align}\] where \(x(t) \in S{R}^n\) is the state, \(\alpha(t) \in S{R}_+\) is the finite-time gain, \(d(x)\) and \(g(t) \in S{R}^n\) are perturbation terms and bounded such that \(\|d(x)\| \le d^*\) and \(\|g(t)\| \le g^*\), and \(f(x)\) is globally Lipschitz. In addition, the origin of the system \(\dot{x}(t) = f(x)\) is a globally asymptotically stable equilibrium point, and there exists a quadratic-type Lyapunov function \(V(x)\) such that \[\begin{aligned} \alpha_1(\|x\|) \le V(x) \le \alpha_2(\|x\|), \label{cond1}\\ \frac{\partial V}{\partial x} \le -c_3 \phi^2(\|x\|), \ \ \ \ \ \|\frac{\partial V}{\partial x}\| \le c_4 \phi(\|x\|), \label{cond2}\end{aligned}\] where \(\alpha_1\) and \(\alpha_2\) are class \(\mathcal{K}\) functions, \(c_3\) and \(c_4\) are positive constants, and the scalar function \(\phi(\|x\|)\) is positive definite and continuous.

[exp_stab] By converse Lyapunov theorem, (see, for example, Theorem 3.8 of ), if the origin of the system \(\dot{x}(t) = f(x)\) is globally exponentially stable, then there is a continuous differentiable function \(V(x)\) that satisfies the inequalities \[\begin{aligned} c_1\|x\|^2 \le V(x) \le c_2\|x\|^2, \label{cond3} \\ \frac{\partial V}{\partial x} \le -c_3 \|x\|^2, \ \ \ \ \ \|\frac{\partial V}{\partial x}\| \le c_4 \|x\|, \label{cond4}\end{aligned}\] for all \(x(t) \in S{R}^n\), and \(c_1, c_2, c_3,\) and \(c_4 \in S{R}_+\). As emphasized in Section 4.2 of , the Lyapunov function satisfying ([cond3]) and ([cond4]) also satisfies ([cond1]) and ([cond2]), but the quadratic-type Lyapunov function may exist even when the origin is not exponentially stable.

[obj1] Generalize the procedure to choose the finite-time gain \(\alpha(t)\) and the time transformation \(\theta(s)\).

[obj2] Prove that \(x(t) \rightarrow 0\) as \(t \rightarrow T\).

[presence] The dynamics form (1) is an error dynamics and can be seen, for examples, in (10) of ; in (18) of [Robustness of FT Distributed Algorithm]; in (15) of [Finite Time + Separation Principle]. However, the dynamics structure in the aforementioned papers applies only to a class of linear systems; and hence, the origins of their nominal systems are globally exponentially stable and readily satisfied ([cond3]) and ([cond4]). The main motivation of this paper is to generalize the problem to a broader class of systems and investigate in detail on how to choose the finite-time gain \(\alpha(t)\).

Choosing Finite-Time Gain and the Time Transformation

In this section, we investigate the relationship between the finite-time gain \(\alpha(t)\) and the time transformation function \(\theta(s)\). The result of this section aims to achieve the Objective [obj1] and serves as a basis to attain Objective 2.

In general, we would like to choose a scalar function as a finite-time gain \(\alpha(t)\) with properties:

  • \(\alpha(t)\) is continuous differentiable on \(t \in [0,T)\).

  • \(\alpha(t) > m \ \ \forall \ t \in [0,T)\) for some \(m > 0\).

  • \(\lim_{t \rightarrow T} \alpha(t) = \infty\).

After choosing \(\alpha(t)\), we define the time transformation function \(\theta(s)\) with properties as shown in the following theorem.

[T1] Given a finite-time gain \(\alpha(t)\) as described above. If the time transformation \(\theta(s) \triangleq t\) is obtained by solving the differential equation in i.) and satisfies the conditions ii.) and iii.) below

  1. \(\frac{dt}{ds} = \frac{d(\theta(s))}{ds} = \frac{1}{\alpha(\theta(s))}\), or \(\alpha(\theta(s)) d(\theta(s)) = ds\), [i]

  2. \(\theta(0) = 0\), [ii]

  3. \(\lim_{s \rightarrow \infty}\theta(s) = T\), [iii]

Then, the following statements hold

  1. \(\theta(s)\) is continuous differentiable and strictly increasing on \(s \in [0,\infty)\). [1]

  2. Let \(h(s) \triangleq\frac{d(\theta(s))}{ds}\), then \(h(s)\) is bounded and \(\lim_{s \rightarrow \infty} h(s) = 0\). [2]

  3. Let \(x(t) = x(\theta(s)) \triangleq\bar{x}(s)\), then the dynamics ([general]) can be rewritten in the stretch time domain \(s \in [0,\infty)\) as \[\begin{aligned} \bar{x}'(s) = f(\bar{x}) + h(s)\Big( d(\bar{x}) + g\big(\theta(s)\big) \Big), \label{general_s}\end{aligned}\] where \(\bar{x}'(s) \triangleq\frac{d\bar{x}}{ds}\). [3]

Proof. For 1.), we first note that the time transformation function \(\theta(s)\) is actually a change of variable mapping the stretch time domain \(s \in [0,\infty)\) to the normal time domain \(t \in [0,T)\). In addition, \(\alpha(t) = \alpha(\theta(s))\). From item [i]), we have \[\begin{aligned} \frac{d(\theta(s))}{ds} = \frac{1}{\alpha(\theta(s))} = \frac{1}{\alpha(t)}, \label{E1}\end{aligned}\] Since \(\alpha(t)\) is continuous differentiable on \(t \in [0,T)\), it is defined on \(t \in [0,T)\); and hence, so is \(1/\alpha(t)\). This implies \(\frac{d(\theta(s))}{ds}\) exists for all \(s \in [0, \infty)\), thus \(\theta(s)\) is continuous differentiable.

In addition, \(\alpha(t) > m \ \ \forall \ t \in [0,T)\) for some \(m > 0\) implies \(\frac{d(\theta(s))}{ds} > 0\). As a result, \(\theta(s)\) is strictly increasing on \(s \in [0,\infty)\). Hence, item 1.) is proved.

For 2.), by definition of \(h(s)\) and ([E1]), we have \[\begin{aligned} h(s)= \frac{1}{\alpha(\theta(s))} = \frac{1}{\alpha(t)}. \label{E2}\end{aligned}\] Since \(\alpha(t) > 0\), \(h(s)\) is bounded by \(h^* \triangleq\frac{1}{\rm block{\scriptstyle -}diag{\rm inf}_{0\le t < T} \big(\alpha(t)\big)}\). In addition, items [ii]) and [iii]) indicates that as \(s\) approaches \(\infty\), \(t\) approaches \(T\), and recall that \(\alpha(t)\) satisfies \(\lim_{t \rightarrow T} \alpha(t) = \infty\). Therefore, we have \[\begin{aligned} \lim_{s \rightarrow \infty} h(s) = \lim_{s \rightarrow \infty} \frac{1}{\alpha\big(\theta(s)\big)} = \lim_{t \rightarrow T} \frac{1}{\alpha(t)} = \big( \lim_{t \rightarrow T} \alpha(t) \big)^{-1} = 0.\end{aligned}\] Indeed, we note that \[\begin{aligned} \int_{0}^{\infty} |h(s)| ds = \int_{0}^{\infty} h(s) ds = \int_{0}^{T} d\big(\theta(s) \big) = \int_{0}^{T} dt = T. \label{L1}\end{aligned}\] Thus, \(h(s) \in \mathcal{L}_1\) on stretch time interval \(s \in [0,\infty)\). Hence, item 2.) is proved.

[whyT] \(h(s)\) is the derivative of \(\theta(s)\); that is, \(h(s) = \frac{d(\theta(s))}{ds}\), so its integral is \(\theta(s) = t\) itself, which makes the above integral equal to \(T\).

For 3.), we have \[\begin{aligned} \bar{x}'(s) = & \frac{d\bar{x}}{ds} = \frac{d\theta(s)}{ds} \frac{d \bar{x}}{d\theta(s)} \nonumber \\ = & \frac{1}{\alpha(\theta(s))} \Big( \alpha(\theta(s)) f(\bar{x}) + d(\bar{x}) + g(\theta(s)) \Big)\nonumber \\ = & f(\bar{x}) + \frac{1}{\alpha(\theta(s))} \Big( d(\bar{x}) + g(\theta(s)) \Big) \nonumber \\ = & f(\bar{x}) + h(s)\Big( d(\bar{x}) + g\big(\theta(s)\big) \Big). \label{Stretch}\end{aligned}\] Hence, item 3.) is proved. \(\hfill \blacksquare\)

Example 1. Consider \(\alpha(t) = \frac{1}{(T-t)(mt+a)}\), where \(m, a>0\). After solving the differential equation in condition i.) of Theorem [T1], we obtain \(\theta(s) = \frac{Ta(e^{(a+mT)s}-1)}{ae^{(a+mT)s} + mT}\) satisfying the conditions ii) and iii).

Let \(a = 0.1\), \(m = 0.1\), and \(T = 5\). Figure [Plot] shows the plots of \(\alpha(t)\), \(\theta(s)\) and \(h(s)\). As we can see from the figure, \(\theta(s)\) is strictly increasing on \(s \in [0,\infty)\). In addition, \(\alpha(t)\) has a critical point (global minimum) at \(t = 2\) where \(\alpha = 1.111\). Note that the plot of \(\theta(s)\) shows that when \(t = 2\), \(s = 2.682\). Furthermore, \(h(s)\) has a critical point (global maximum) at \(s = 2.682\) where \(h = \frac{1}{1.111} = 0.9\). This justifies the item 2.) of Theorem [T1]!

Figure [Plot2] shows plots of the family of \(\theta(s)\) above as \(a\) is increasing while \(m\) is fixed, and \(m\) is increasing as \(a\) is fixed, where the arrow pointing in the increasing direction of \(a\) and \(m\). In addition, the time transformation function \(\theta(s) = T(1 - e^{-s})\) (dash line) is also plotted for comparison purpose. We should also note that \(a\) affects the initial gain of \(\alpha(t)\) while \(m\) affects how fast \(\alpha(t)\) decay during the transient stage.

The Analysis

In this section, we uses the result of Section 2 in order to achieve Objective [obj2]. Specifically, we prove \(x(t) \rightarrow 0\) as \(t \rightarrow T\) utilizing the information on the finite-time gain \(\alpha(t)\) and the time transformation function \(\theta(s)\) given in Theorem [T1].

We first recall that, by Theorem 1, the dynamics ([general]) can be rewritten in the stretch time domain \(s \in [0,\infty)\) as \[\begin{aligned} \bar{x}'(s) = f(\bar{x}) + h(s)\Big( d(\bar{x}) + g\big(\theta(s)\big) \Big). \label{general_s2}\end{aligned}\] Define \(u(s) \triangleq h(s)\Big( d(\bar{x}) + g(\theta(s) \Big) \in S{R}^n\). We note that \(h(s), d(\bar{x})\) and \(g(\theta(s))\) are bounded by \(h^*, d^*\) and \(g^*\), respectively; and hence \(u(s)\) is also bounded such that \(\|u(s)\| \le u^* \triangleq h^*(d^*+g^*)\). We now rewrite ([general_s2]) in the form \[\begin{aligned} \bar{x}'(s) = f(\bar{x}) + u(s). \label{general_s3}\end{aligned}\]

Utilizing the same Lyapunov function of the nominal system \(\dot{x}(t) = f(x)\), which satisfies ([cond1]) and ([cond2]), and taking its derivative with respect to \(s \in [0,\infty)\) along the trajectories of ([general_s3]), we have \[\begin{align} V'(\bar{x}) &=& \frac{\partial V}{\partial \bar{x}} \Big( f(\bar{x}) + u(s) \Big)\nonumber \\ &=& \frac{\partial V}{\partial \bar{x}} f(\bar{x}) + \frac{\partial V}{\partial \bar{x}} u(s) \nonumber \\ &\le& -c_3 \phi^2(\|\bar{x}\|) + c_4 \phi(\|\bar{x}\|)\|u(s)\| \nonumber \\ &=& -c_3 \phi^2(\|\bar{x}\|) +\theta \phi^2(\|\bar{x}\|) -\theta \phi^2(\|\bar{x}\|) + c_4 \phi(\|\bar{x}\|)\|u(s)\|\nonumber \\ &\le& -(c_3-\theta)\phi^2(\|\bar{x}\|), \ \ \ \forall \ \|x\| \ge \phi^{-1}\left(\frac{c_4 \|u(s)\|}{\theta}\right), \label{Lyap}\end{align}\] where \(\theta \in (0,c_3)\), and the third inequality comes from ([cond2]). By Theorem 4.6 of , the system ([general_s3]) is input-to-state stable. We note that input-to-state stability implies that when the input converges to zeros as \(s \rightarrow \infty\), so does the state. From Theorem [T1], \(\lim_{s \rightarrow \infty} h(s) = 0\); and hence, \(\lim_{s \rightarrow \infty} u(s) = 0\). As a result, \(\bar{x}(s) \rightarrow 0\) as \(s \rightarrow \infty\). Finally, since \(t\) approaches \(T\) as \(s\) approaches \(\infty\), \(\lim_{t \rightarrow T}x(t) = 0\).

[application] The result of this section shows that although the dynamics ([general]) is subject to perturbations, its state can converge to zero in a user-defined finite time \(T\) under the finite-time gain \(\alpha(t)\). Therefore, for time-critical tasks, if we design the controller for the system such that its error dynamics can be written in the form of ([general]), then its stability is guaranteed.

[local] If the origin of the nominal system \(\dot{x}(t) = f(x)\) is an asymptotically stable equilibrium point, where \(f(x)\) is locally Lipschitz on a domain \(D \subset S{R}^n\), and there exists a quadratic-type Lyapunov function \(V(x)\) satisfying the condition ([cond1]) and ([cond2]), then the result of this section still hold locally within the region of attraction \(R_A \subset D\).
